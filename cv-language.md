# CV Language Guide

Use this guide for every generic or tailored CV produced from this repository.

## Core standard

Write precise, evidence-based professional English. Optimise for a recruiter who is scanning quickly and an ATS that is extracting plain text. Strong language comes from specificity, not exaggeration.

## Voice and grammar

- Use British English by default: `prioritised`, `normalising`, and `modelling`.
- Switch variants only when the user requests it or the target employer clearly uses another convention.
- Omit first-person pronouns in CV bullets.
- Use present tense for continuing responsibilities and past tense for completed achievements and previous roles.
- Begin bullets with a strong verb and keep grammatical structure parallel.
- Prefer direct sentences over noun-heavy corporate language.

## Evidence before adjectives

Prefer:

> Architected a unified subscription layer across Stripe, Apple App Store, and Google Play.

Avoid:

> Highly skilled engineer who successfully implemented an innovative payment solution.

Do not use unsupported superlatives such as `expert`, `world-class`, `best-in-class`, `exceptional`, or `industry-leading`.

## Bullet construction

A strong bullet usually contains two or more of these elements:

1. Action: what the candidate did.
2. Scope: product, system, team, users, platforms, or data involved.
3. Technical method: architecture, technologies, or constraints.
4. Result: measurable or qualitative impact.
5. Ownership: what the candidate personally owned versus collaborated on.

Use a result only when it is known. A technically substantial scope or constraint can be strong evidence without a metric.

## Metrics

- Use exact or approximate metrics only when the candidate confirms them.
- Label approximate figures honestly.
- Never derive a metric from an assumption.
- Do not force weak metrics into a CV merely to satisfy an action-result formula.
- Respect a candidate's preference not to disclose commercially sensitive outcomes.

## Titles, leadership, and collaboration

- Use the official title in the role heading.
- Describe unofficial leadership in bullets: `Provided technical leadership`, `Mentored`, `Coordinated`, or `Led the implementation`.
- Do not convert lead-like responsibility into `Team Lead`, `Manager`, `Head of`, or `CTO` without confirmation.
- Distinguish `built`, `co-built`, `contributed to`, `integrated`, and `supported` accurately.
- Name collaboration boundaries when they prevent overclaiming.

## Technical language

- Use official capitalisation: Ruby on Rails, PostgreSQL, JavaScript, TypeScript, GitHub Actions, App Store, Google Play, and GoCardless.
- Prefer the specific technology when it improves credibility or matches the job description.
- Avoid long technology inventories inside experience bullets when a skills section can carry them.
- Explain specialised architecture in plain language before adding jargon.
- Do not call deterministic algorithms `AI`.
- Mark planned or experimental features as `planned`, `in progress`, or `prototype`.

## Tailoring

- Mirror relevant terminology from the job description only when supported by the career inventory.
- Prioritise the most relevant accomplishments rather than adding more content.
- Do not repeat the same evidence in the profile, experience, and project sections.
- Keep a general profile broad; make a tailored profile address the target role's highest-value needs.
- Preserve truthful seniority. Do not rewrite `Mid` as `Senior` because a vacancy requests senior experience.

## Profile summaries

- Keep the profile to two or three concise lines.
- Lead with professional identity and strongest evidence.
- Mention years of experience only when dates support the calculation and the number adds value.
- Avoid objectives such as `seeking a challenging position`.
- Avoid personality claims such as `hard-working`, `passionate`, or `team player` unless demonstrated by evidence elsewhere.

## Readability and ATS compatibility

- Prefer conventional headings and plain text.
- Keep bullets to roughly one or two rendered lines where possible.
- Use abbreviations only when commonly recognised or spell them out on first use.
- Avoid icons as the only representation of contact details.
- Avoid skill bars, star ratings, keyword clouds, text embedded in images, and multi-column reading orders that extract incorrectly.
- Keep dates and titles consistent throughout the document.

## Content to avoid by default

- Photograph, date of birth, age, marital status, religion, gender, identity numbers, or full street address.
- Salary expectations and references.
- Generic soft-skill lists without evidence.
- Obsolete or irrelevant technologies included only to fill space.
- Private employer information, credentials, source code, or confidential metrics.
- Links that are broken, private, or unrelated to the application.

## Final language check

Before delivery, confirm that:

- Every claim is supported by `career_inventory.md` or newly confirmed by the user.
- Current and previous roles use the correct tense.
- Official and unofficial titles are clearly distinguished.
- Technology names are spelled consistently.
- No bullet overstates personal ownership.
- No planned feature reads as shipped.
- The strongest evidence appears in the upper half of the first page.
- The CV reads naturally aloud and does not sound generated or inflated.
