# Professional CV Guidance

Use this reference to inform non-academic, job-specific CV decisions. It synthesises public guidance from official university career services and the UK National Careers Service; it does not reproduce their guides.

No CV format can guarantee an interview or a hiring outcome. Apply these practices as evidence-informed defaults, then follow the employer's explicit application instructions and the repository's accuracy rules.

## Source consensus

### Tailor to the vacancy

- Start with the job description, essential criteria, and employer instructions.
- Select experiences that demonstrate the requested skills and outcomes.
- Reorder and shorten content for relevance instead of presenting the full career inventory.
- Use job-description terminology only when it truthfully describes verified experience.

Oxford, MIT, Harvard, and the National Careers Service all recommend adapting each CV or resume to the target position rather than sending an unchanged master document.

### Make relevant information easy to find

- Use conventional, descriptive section headings.
- Prefer reverse chronological order within experience and education sections unless a different structure clearly serves the role.
- Put the strongest relevant evidence where it will be seen early.
- Use a consistent hierarchy, restrained emphasis, readable spacing, and concise bullet points.
- Keep employer, title, location, and dates visually consistent across roles.

Oxford recommends one or two full pages for most non-academic CVs and notes that recruiters may scan quickly. This repository prefers one page and uses two only when additional relevant evidence materially improves the application.

### Demonstrate contribution and impact

For each high-value bullet, combine as many of these as the evidence supports:

1. The action or responsibility.
2. The product, system, users, team, or operational scope.
3. The method, technology, decision, or constraint.
4. The outcome or resulting capability.
5. The candidate's ownership and collaboration boundary.

Quantify scale or impact only when the candidate confirms the figure. A specific technical scope, difficult constraint, or qualitative outcome is stronger than an invented or weak metric.

### Write clearly and specifically

- Prefer active, direct language over generic adjectives and corporate jargon.
- Begin bullets with precise verbs and keep their grammar parallel.
- Use present tense for ongoing responsibilities and past tense for completed work.
- Avoid first-person pronouns in bullets.
- Spell out uncommon abbreviations on first use.
- Proofread names, dates, spelling, grammar, and punctuation.

Harvard advises specific, active, fact-based language. Oxford recommends simple language and tense consistency. The National Careers Service emphasises clarity, brevity, and proofreading.

### Preserve ATS readability

- Use selectable text, ordinary section headings, and a logical single-column reading order.
- Use a clear font at a readable size; do not shrink content merely to force a page limit.
- Use bullets and whitespace to support scanning.
- Keep contact details visible as text rather than icons alone.
- Avoid text embedded in images, skill ratings, decorative charts, and complex reading orders.
- Extract text from the final PDF and verify that headings, dates, contact details, and important terms remain in a sensible order.

Official guidance consistently prioritises clarity, scannability, consistent formatting, and immediately visible relevant information. ATS-specific claims made by commercial scoring tools should not override factual accuracy or readable writing.

## Market and application differences

- Use A4 and British English by default for UK or Cyprus applications.
- Use Letter size and the employer's spelling conventions for North American applications.
- Treat a professional UK CV and a US resume as comparable short application documents for this workflow.
- Treat an academic CV as a separate document type: it can be longer and may require publications, teaching, research, grants, presentations, and references.
- Follow an employer's requested fields, file format, and page limit even when they differ from these defaults.

Do not automatically copy market-specific advice about photographs, full addresses, nationality, references, or other personal information. Apply the repository's privacy rules and ask the user when local convention or an employer instruction makes the choice material.

## Agent application checklist

Before drafting:

- Identify the vacancy's essential and preferred criteria.
- Map each important criterion to verified evidence.
- Separate strong matches, partial matches, and genuine gaps.
- Ask focused questions only where answers could materially improve accuracy or relevance.
- Confirm time-sensitive contact details, current roles, metrics, and launch status.

While drafting:

- Choose content for relevance, evidence strength, and distinctiveness.
- Avoid repeating the same claim in the profile, experience, and projects sections.
- Preserve official titles and accurate ownership language.
- Keep planned, experimental, and in-progress work labelled correctly.
- Use the selected template's structure without sacrificing readability.

Before delivery:

- Verify every claim against `career_inventory.md` or a newly confirmed answer.
- Confirm the strongest evidence appears early.
- Remove unsupported adjectives, duplicated evidence, irrelevant history, and keyword stuffing.
- Compile, render, visually inspect, and extract text from the PDF.
- Correct all clipping, overlap, broken glyphs, poor wrapping, inconsistent spacing, and extraction-order problems.
